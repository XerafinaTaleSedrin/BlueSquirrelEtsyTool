# Competitive Analysis Framework

**Purpose**: Systematic approach to analyzing competitors and existing solutions.

This framework defines how to research competitors thoroughly, understand their strengths and weaknesses, and identify differentiation opportunities.

---

## When to Do Deep Competitive Analysis

**Quick Analysis** (for all ideas):
- Identify 5-8 competitors during market research
- Basic feature/pricing comparison
- Included in `02_market-research.md`

**Deep Analysis** (for promising ideas):
- Detailed analysis of 2-3 primary competitors
- Create separate files in `supporting-research/competitor-XX_[name].md`
- Do this after market research shows opportunity

---

## Competitor Research Structure

For each major competitor, research these areas:

### 1. Company Overview

**Basic Information**:
- Company name and website
- Founded when? (age of company)
- Headquarters location
- Company size (employees via LinkedIn)
- Funding status (bootstrapped, VC-backed, public)
- Funding amount (if VC-backed, via Crunchbase)

**Why it matters**:
- Age indicates market maturity and learning curve
- Size indicates resources and threat level
- Funding shows runway and growth intentions
- Location can indicate regulatory constraints

**Sources**:
- Company website (About page)
- LinkedIn company page (employee count)
- Crunchbase (funding data)
- PitchBook or AngelList
- News articles about funding

---

### 2. Product/Service Offering

**Core Offering**:
- What problem do they solve?
- How do they solve it?
- What's their unique value proposition?
- What's their core product/service?

**Features & Capabilities**:
- Major features (list top 10-15)
- Unique features (what only they have)
- Missing features (what they don't offer)
- Platform (web, mobile, desktop, API)

**User Experience**:
- Ease of use (try it if possible)
- Onboarding experience
- Design quality
- Performance and speed

**Technology Stack** (if discoverable):
- Built With tool
- Job postings (reveal tech stack)
- Engineering blog
- API documentation

**Why it matters**:
- Shows what customers expect as baseline
- Identifies gaps or areas of over/under-serving
- Reveals technical complexity and requirements

**Sources**:
- Product website and demo
- Free trial or freemium version
- YouTube demo videos
- Product Hunt reviews
- G2/Capterra feature lists

---

### 3. Pricing & Business Model

**Pricing Structure**:
- Free tier (if any)
- Paid tiers (all pricing levels)
- Enterprise pricing (if available)
- Add-ons or upsells

**Business Model**:
- Subscription (monthly/annual)
- One-time purchase
- Transaction fees
- Freemium with upgrades
- Advertising
- Marketplace commission

**Value Metrics**:
- How do they charge? (per user, per feature, per usage)
- What drives upgrade decisions?
- What's included in each tier?

**Pricing Psychology**:
- Anchor pricing (highest tier shown first?)
- Discounting strategy (annual vs. monthly)
- Free trial duration
- Money-back guarantee?

**Why it matters**:
- Sets customer expectations for pricing
- Reveals willingness to pay in the market
- Shows unit economics and margins
- Identifies pricing gaps or opportunities

**Sources**:
- Pricing page
- Sign-up flow
- Sales calls (if available)
- Competitor analysis sites
- Customer reviews mentioning pricing

---

### 4. Target Market & Positioning

**Target Customer**:
- Who is this for? (demographics, firmographics)
- Primary use case
- Secondary use cases
- Customer segments served

**Market Position**:
- Budget option vs. premium
- Generalist vs. specialist
- Self-service vs. white-glove
- SMB vs. enterprise

**Messaging & Branding**:
- Headline/value prop on homepage
- Key messaging themes
- Brand voice and tone
- Visual identity

**Why it matters**:
- Shows market segmentation opportunities
- Reveals positioning gaps
- Indicates where competition is fiercest
- Helps identify differentiation angles

**Sources**:
- Website homepage
- Marketing materials
- Case studies and testimonials
- Ad copy (Facebook Ad Library, Google Ads)
- LinkedIn company description

---

### 5. Go-to-Market Strategy

**Customer Acquisition**:
- How do they get customers?
- Organic (SEO, content marketing)
- Paid (ads, sponsorships)
- Sales team (outbound)
- Partnerships
- Referrals or virality

**Marketing Channels**:
- Content marketing (blog, guides)
- Social media presence
- Paid advertising
- Events and conferences
- PR and media coverage

**Sales Process**:
- Self-service (sign up and go)
- Sales-assisted (demo call required)
- Enterprise sales (custom quotes)

**Why it matters**:
- Shows customer acquisition cost (CAC) level
- Reveals how to reach target customers
- Indicates barriers to entry
- Shows what marketing approaches work

**Sources**:
- Website traffic sources (SimilarWeb)
- Social media followings
- Blog post frequency and topics
- Job postings (sales, marketing roles)
- Facebook Ad Library (active ads)

---

### 6. Traction & Growth

**Market Share**:
- Number of customers (if disclosed)
- Website traffic (SimilarWeb, Ahrefs estimates)
- App downloads (if mobile app)
- Social media followers
- Employee count growth (LinkedIn)

**Growth Indicators**:
- Traffic trend (growing/flat/declining)
- Funding rounds (recent raises indicate growth)
- Team expansion (hiring or laying off)
- Product development pace (changelog activity)
- Market presence (mentions, reviews increasing)

**Customer Sentiment**:
- Reviews on G2, Capterra, Trustpilot
- App store ratings
- Social media sentiment
- Customer support responsiveness

**Why it matters**:
- Shows if market is viable
- Indicates strength of competition
- Reveals customer satisfaction
- Signals market trends

**Sources**:
- SimilarWeb traffic estimates
- G2/Capterra reviews and ratings
- App store reviews
- LinkedIn company page employee changes
- Crunchbase funding timeline

---

### 7. Strengths & Weaknesses

**Strengths** (what they do well):
- Competitive advantages
- Strong features or capabilities
- Brand recognition
- Network effects
- Technical moat
- Capital/resources

**Weaknesses** (where they struggle):
- Customer complaints (from reviews)
- Missing features (from reviews)
- Poor UX or performance
- High pricing without justification
- Slow innovation
- Limited platform support

**Vulnerabilities** (attack vectors):
- Over-serving certain segments (expensive/complex)
- Under-serving other segments (too simple/limited)
- Legacy technology (slow, hard to change)
- Reliance on specific platform or partnership
- Negative reviews on specific issues

**Why it matters**:
- Identifies differentiation opportunities
- Shows where to compete or avoid
- Reveals customer pain points
- Guides product positioning

**Sources**:
- Product reviews (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot)
- Reddit discussions
- Twitter/X complaints
- Quora questions
- Support forums
- Competitor comparison articles

---

### 8. Competitive Response Risk

**If you compete, how will they respond?**

**Likely Responses**:
- Ignore (if too small to notice)
- Copy (if easy to replicate your differentiation)
- Acquire (if strategic fit)
- Compete aggressively (price cuts, marketing spend)

**Defensive Moats**:
- What prevents them from easily copying you?
- Speed to market?
- Specialized expertise?
- Unique partnerships or data?
- Different business model?
- Niche focus (not worth their time)?

**Why it matters**:
- Anticipate competitive dynamics
- Build defensibility from the start
- Choose battles wisely
- Plan for scenarios

## Competitive Positioning Matrix

Create a 2x2 matrix to visualize competitive landscape:

**Axes examples**:
- Price (low to high) vs. Feature Set (simple to complex)
- Ease of Use (simple to complex) vs. Power (basic to advanced)
- Market (SMB to Enterprise) vs. Specialization (generalist to niche)

**How to use**:
- Plot all competitors on the matrix
- Identify crowded quadrants (high competition)
- Identify empty quadrants (opportunities or non-viable)
- Position your idea in the least crowded, viable quadrant

## Feature Comparison Table

**Format**:
```markdown
| Feature | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C | Your Idea |
|---------|--------------|--------------|--------------|-----------|
| Feature 1 | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ |
| Feature 2 | Basic | Advanced | Basic | Advanced |
| Feature 3 | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ (unique) |
| Price (lowest tier) | $X/mo | $Y/mo | $Z/mo | $?/mo |
```

**Include**:
- Core features (baseline expectations)
- Differentiating features (what makes them unique)
- Missing features (gaps in market)
- Your planned features (where you'll compete)

---

## Pricing Comparison Table

**Format**:
```markdown
| Competitor | Free Tier | Starter | Professional | Enterprise |
|------------|-----------|---------|--------------|------------|
| Competitor A | ✗ | $X/mo | $Y/mo | Custom |
| Competitor B | 14-day trial | $A/mo | $B/mo | $C/mo |
| Competitor C | Freemium | $P/mo | $Q/mo | Custom |
| Market Range | - | $X-$P | $Y-$Q | - |
```

**Insights to draw**:
- What's the pricing range?
- What's included in each tier?
- Where are pricing gaps?
- What's the value metric?

## SWOT Summary for Each Competitor

Quick SWOT for each major competitor:

**Strengths**: What they do well
**Weaknesses**: Where they struggle
**Opportunities**: How they could improve (threats to you)
**Threats**: What could hurt them (opportunities for you)

## Differentiation Opportunities

Based on competitive analysis, identify:

**Feature Gaps**:
- What features do customers want that no one offers?
- Evidence: Reviews, forum discussions, feature requests

**Pricing Gaps**:
- Is there a price point not served?
- Too expensive for small customers?
- Too cheap for enterprise needs?

**Market Gaps**:
- Segments underserved?
- Geographic regions not covered?
- Industries or verticals overlooked?

**Experience Gaps**:
- Poor UX across competitors?
- Slow onboarding?
- Bad customer support?
- Complex setup?

**Business Model Gaps**:
- Different pricing model opportunity?
- Self-service vs. full-service?
- Platform vs. service?

---

## Red Flags from Competitive Analysis

**Market Saturation**:
- 10+ competitors with similar offerings
- No clear differentiation between them
- Price wars happening
- Declining margins

**Winner-Take-Most**:
- One player dominates (>50% market share)
- Strong network effects
- High switching costs
- Hard to displace incumbent

**Dead Bodies**:
- Many failed startups in the space
- Acquisitions that shut down products
- Pivots away from this market
- Pattern of failures (research why they failed)

**Low Willingness to Pay**:
- Most competitors are free
- Paid tiers have low pricing ($5-10/mo)
- Freemium with low upgrade rates
- Ad-supported models (signals low monetization)

## Key Principles

**Be thorough but efficient**:
- Quick scan for all competitors
- Deep dive only on top 2-3
- Time-box research (don't spiral)

**Try before you judge**:
- Sign up for free trials
- Use the product if possible
- Experience the customer journey

**Listen to customers**:
- Reviews reveal truth
- People are honest about pain points
- Negative reviews show opportunities

**Look for patterns**:
- What do all competitors do? (table stakes)
- What does no one do? (opportunity or non-viable)
- What failed? (learn from mistakes)

**Stay objective**:
- Don't dismiss competitors as "bad"
- They're solving a real problem
- Respect what they've built
- Learn from their successes and failures

---

## Next Steps After Competitive Analysis

**If low competition**:
- Validate that market exists (not just empty)
- Understand why it's empty (opportunity or graveyard)

**If moderate competition**:
- Identify clear differentiation angle
- Validate that differentiation matters to customers

**If high competition**:
- Find niche or underserved segment
- Consider if it's worth entering crowded market
- Need strong unique advantage to justify

Competitive analysis should inform whether to proceed and how to differentiate.
