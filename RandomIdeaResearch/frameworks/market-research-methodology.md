# Market Research Methodology

**Purpose**: Systematic approach to researching market opportunities for business ideas.

This framework defines how to research markets thoroughly, identify competitors, assess opportunity, and surface critical questions that determine viability.

---

## Research Framework

### 1. Problem Definition

**Questions to Answer**:
- What problem does this solve?
- Who experiences this problem?
- How acute is the pain point? (nice-to-have vs. must-have)
- How do people solve it today?
- What's inadequate about current solutions?

**Evidence to Gather**:
- Customer complaints about existing solutions
- Forum discussions about the problem
- Reddit threads, product reviews, support tickets
- Social media mentions of frustration
- Google search volume for "[problem] solutions"

**Red Flag**: If you can't find evidence that people actively complain about or search for this problem, the pain point may not be acute enough.

---

### 2. Market Landscape

#### A. Competitive Analysis

**Direct Competitors** - Solving the exact same problem:
- Who are they?
- What do they offer?
- How big are they? (employees, funding, customers)
- What do they charge?
- What are their strengths/weaknesses?

**Indirect Competitors** - Alternative solutions to the same problem:
- What other ways do people solve this?
- Manual processes being used?
- Workarounds or hacks?
- Adjacent products being repurposed?

**Adjacent Solutions** - Partial overlaps:
- Who solves similar problems?
- What features overlap?
- What's different?

**Search Patterns**:
- "[problem] app"
- "[problem] software"
- "[problem] service"
- "best [problem] solutions 2025"
- "alternatives to [known competitor]"
- "[industry] tools"
- "[problem] startups"

**Sources**:
- Google search
- Product Hunt
- Crunchbase
- Y Combinator companies
- AngelList
- LinkedIn company search
- App stores (iOS/Android)
- Chrome Web Store
- Reddit r/SaaS, r/startups, r/Entrepreneur

---

#### B. Market Sizing

**TAM (Total Addressable Market)**:
- How many potential customers exist globally?
- What's the maximum revenue possible?

**SAM (Serviceable Available Market)**:
- How many could you realistically reach?
- Geographic, language, or regulatory constraints?

**SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)**:
- What market share is realistic for a new entrant?
- What could you capture in first 1-3 years?

**Market Size Tiers**:
- **Large**: $1B+ TAM, millions of potential customers
- **Medium**: $100M-$1B TAM, hundreds of thousands of customers
- **Small**: $10M-$100M TAM, tens of thousands of customers
- **Micro**: <$10M TAM, very specialized niche

**Information Sources**:
- Industry reports (IBISWorld, Gartner, Forrester - free summaries)
- Government statistics (Census, BLS, industry data)
- Public company earnings calls and 10-K filings
- Market research firm estimates (Statista, eMarketer)
- Google Trends data
- LinkedIn company employee counts
- SimilarWeb traffic estimates
- App store download estimates

**Red Flag**: If you can't find ANY market size data, the market may be too niche or too new. Estimate from first principles but note high uncertainty.

---

### 3. Customer Research

**Customer Characteristics**:
- Demographics (age, location, income, education)
- Psychographics (values, interests, behavior patterns)
- Current solutions they use
- Pain points and needs
- Willingness to pay signals
- Where they congregate online/offline

**Customer Segments**:
- Primary target (who needs this most urgently?)
- Secondary opportunities (who else could benefit?)
- Tertiary markets (possible future expansion)

**Evidence Sources**:
- Reddit discussions in relevant subreddits
- Product review sites (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot)
- Community forums (Discord, Slack communities, Facebook groups)
- Social media (Twitter/X, LinkedIn)
- Support forums for existing solutions
- Quora questions
- YouTube reviews and comments

**Key Signals to Look For**:
- "I wish there was a tool that..."
- "Why doesn't [solution] do [feature]?"
- "I'm stuck doing this manually..."
- "We pay $X for [tool] but it doesn't..."
- Pricing discussions (what people actually pay)

---

### 4. Differentiation Analysis

**Critical Questions**:
- What makes existing solutions inadequate?
- Where are the gaps in the market?
- What could make this idea different/better?
- **Why hasn't this been done already?** ← MOST IMPORTANT QUESTION

**Possible Answers to "Why Not?"**:
- ✓ Technology just became available (enabling tech is new)
- ✓ Market shift created new need (behavior change, regulation, trend)
- ✓ Existing players are legacy and slow to adapt
- ✓ Niche is too small for large players but viable for solo/small team
- ✓ Requires domain expertise that's rare
- ✗ It's been tried and failed (research why)
- ✗ Economics don't work (too expensive to build/run)
- ✗ Market isn't actually big enough
- ✗ Technical barriers are too high

**Differentiation Opportunities**:
- Better user experience
- Lower price point
- Faster/simpler solution
- Specialized for niche segment
- Different business model
- Better customer support
- Platform integration
- Modern technology stack

**Red Flag**: If the answer to "why hasn't this been done?" is unclear or concerning, this is a critical risk that may make the idea unviable.

---

### 5. Market Trends

**Growth Indicators**:
- Market growing, stable, or shrinking?
- Google Trends trajectory (up, flat, down)
- New competitors entering or exiting?
- Funding activity (startups being funded in this space?)
- Technology trends (what's enabling or threatening this?)

**Trend Categories**:

**Technology Trends**:
- New platforms or capabilities
- API availability
- Infrastructure improvements
- AI/ML advancements
- Mobile/web technology

**Regulatory Trends**:
- New laws or regulations
- Compliance requirements
- Privacy changes (GDPR, CCPA, etc.)
- Industry standards

**Social/Cultural Trends**:
- Behavior shifts
- Remote work, gig economy, etc.
- Generational preferences
- Sustainability, health trends

**Economic Trends**:
- Recession/growth impact
- Industry consolidation
- Investment appetite
- Customer spending patterns

**Research Sources**:
- Google Trends
- CB Insights reports
- TechCrunch, The Information, etc.
- Industry analyst reports
- LinkedIn "on the rise" data
- Twitter/X trending topics in industry

## Research Quality Standards

Every market research file should include:

### Minimum Requirements:
- ✓ **5-8 direct/indirect competitors** identified with names and URLs
- ✓ **Evidence of market size** (even rough estimates with sources)
- ✓ **Customer segment definition** (who this is for)
- ✓ **Pricing data** from at least 3 existing solutions
- ✓ **Trend analysis** (growing/stable/declining with evidence)
- ✓ **Honest assessment** of "why hasn't this been done?"
- ✓ **All sources documented** (URLs, reports, dates)

### Quality Indicators:
- **Excellent**: 8+ competitors analyzed, multiple customer segments identified, clear differentiation opportunities, pricing tiers documented, trend data with sources
- **Good**: 5-7 competitors, primary customer segment defined, some differentiation ideas, pricing ranges noted, trend direction indicated
- **Needs Work**: <5 competitors, vague customer definition, no pricing data, no trend analysis, missing "why not" question

## Critical Questions Framework

### Existence Check
1. **Does this already exist?**
   - Search exhaustively
   - Check adjacent solutions
   - Look for dead startups (what killed them?)

2. **If it exists, why isn't it dominant?**
   - Poor execution?
   - Wrong timing?
   - Economics don't work?
   - Market too small?

3. **If it doesn't exist, why not?**
   - This is often the MOST important question
   - Be skeptical of "no one thought of this"
   - Usually there's a reason

### Market Validation
1. **Are people actively looking for solutions?**
   - Google search volume
   - Forum discussions frequency
   - Product review site activity

2. **Are people paying for existing solutions?**
   - Proof of willingness to pay
   - What price points exist?
   - How much are similar solutions?

3. **What's the customer acquisition cost?**
   - How do competitors acquire customers?
   - Organic, paid, sales team?
   - CAC signals for your approach

### Competitive Moat
1. **What prevents easy replication?**
   - Network effects?
   - Data advantages?
   - Brand/reputation?
   - Technology barriers?

2. **What advantages could a new entrant have?**
   - Modern tech stack?
   - Specialized expertise?
   - Niche focus?
   - Different business model?

3. **What switching costs exist?**
   - Lock-in factors?
   - Integration complexity?
   - Data migration challenges?

### Key Sections Required:

1. **Executive Summary**
   - Does this already exist?
   - Competitive intensity (low/medium/high)
   - Market size tier
   - Key finding (opportunity or red flags)

2. **Competitive Landscape**
   - Direct competitors (with URLs, pricing, analysis)
   - Indirect competitors (alternative approaches)
   - Market leader analysis

3. **Market Size & Trends**
   - TAM/SAM/SOM estimates with sources

   - Enabling trends
   - Threatening trends

4. **Customer Segments**
   - Primary target customer
   - Secondary opportunities
   - Customer acquisition channels
   - Willingness to pay signals

5. **Differentiation Analysis**
   - Gaps in existing solutions
   - Potential differentiation approaches

6. **Red Flags**
   - Reasons for concern

   - Failed attempts
   - Structural challenges

7. **Opportunity Signals**
   - Underserved segments
   - Emerging needs
   - Technology enablers
   - Positive trends

8. **Sources**
   - All URLs and references with dates

## Red Flags to Watch For

**Market Red Flags**:
- Can't find evidence of the problem being discussed
- No one searching for solutions (low Google Trends volume)
- Market declining or stagnant
- Dozens of competitors with low differentiation
- Recent high-profile failures in the space
- Regulatory barriers

**Competition Red Flags**:
- Well-funded incumbents with network effects
- Free alternatives that work well enough
- Giants (Google, Amazon, Microsoft) entering the space
- Race to the bottom on pricing

**Customer Red Flags**:
- Can't identify who the customer is
- Customer acquisition cost higher than lifetime value
- Long sales cycles with small deal sizes
- No willingness to pay (everything is free)

**Timing Red Flags**:
- Too early (market not ready, technology not mature)
- Too late (market saturated, consolidating)
- Trend peaked (fading interest)

---

## Key Philosophy

**Be skeptically thorough**: The goal is to find reasons why an idea WON'T work as much as reasons it will. Better to discover problems during research than after commitment.

**Honest assessment over confirmation bias**: Don't research to validate the idea, research to understand reality.

**Sources matter**: Document everything. Your future self will want to know where data came from.

**"Why not?" is critical**: If you can't answer why this hasn't been done convincingly, that's a major risk.

---

## Next Steps After Market Research

Based on findings:
- **Strong opportunity signals** → Proceed to feasibility analysis
- **Mixed signals** → Do additional targeted research on uncertainties
- **Major red flags** → Consider pivoting or no-go before investing more time
- **Unclear** → Define specific questions and research them

Market research should give you enough information to decide whether to invest time in deep feasibility analysis or to pivot/stop.
