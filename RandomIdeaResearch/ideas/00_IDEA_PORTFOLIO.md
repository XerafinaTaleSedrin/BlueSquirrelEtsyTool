### Common Red Flags Discovered
*As ideas are evaluated, patterns will emerge here:*

- **API/Platform Dependency Risk**: Ideas that depend on third-party APIs (eBay, Poshmark) face restrictions, deprecation risk, and data access limitations
- **Capital-Intensive Consumer Apps**: Mobile apps competing with funded startups require $50K-$100K+ investment and 18-24 month runway
- **"Free Alternative" Problem**: Consumer apps competing against free tools (Google Lens) need 10x better value proposition to justify subscription
- **Competitive Timing Risk**: Emerging categories attracting multiple funded startups (2025 = year of thrift scanner apps) compress window of opportunity

### Promising Opportunity Patterns
*As ideas are evaluated, successful patterns will emerge here:*

- **Niche Specialist Strategy**: Category-specific tools (Rebag's handbag-only scanner) can dominate by going deep vs. broad
- **Validated Markets with Execution Gaps**: Markets where demand exists but current solutions have clear flaws (speed, accuracy, data quality)
- **B2B Over B2C for Bootstrap**: Professional users ($50-300/month) vs consumers ($10-20/month) offer better unit economics and lower CAC
- **Existing Tool Ecosystems**: Resellers already pay for tools (ScoutIQ $14-44/month, Vendoo $15-60/month) - willingness to pay is proven
- **Leverage Existing Capabilities**: Educational Logic Puzzles leverages puzzle generation work from Logic Puzzle App - cross-pollination reduces effort
- **Low-Risk Validation First**: TPT provides low-cost validation before committing to activity book production
- **Fragmented Markets with No Dominant Player**: "Curriculum-aligned logic puzzles" has no clear leader despite market demand

## Portfolio Management Guidelines

### When to Research an Idea
✓ Genuinely curious about the problem
✓ Have some initial evidence of market need
✓ Could realistically pursue if it checks out
✓ Haven't researched something very similar recently

### When to Skip Research
✗ Already know it exists and is saturated
✗ Clearly outside your capability/interest
✗ Just saw a headline and got excited (let it simmer)
✗ Too similar to recently rejected idea

### Priority Assignment
**High Priority**: Strong initial signal, clear personal fit, time-sensitive opportunity
**Medium Priority**: Interesting, worth researching, but no urgency
**Low Priority**: Curious but uncertain, exploratory, nice-to-know

### Healthy Portfolio Mix
Aim for diversity:
- Different markets (B2B, B2C, niches)
- Different business models (SaaS, marketplace, service)
- Different risk levels (some safe, some ambitious)
- Different stages (some early research, some near decision)

## Idea Lifecycle Expectations

### Typical Timeline (can vary widely)
- **Brainstorm → Market Research**: 1-3 days
- **Market Research → Feasibility**: 3-7 days
- **Feasibility → Decision**: 1-3 days
- **Total: Idea to Decision**: 5-14 days

### Red Flags in Process
- **Stuck in Brainstorm >1 week**: Either research it or archive it
- **Stuck in Market Research >2 weeks**: Set research deadline, make decision with available data
- **Stuck in Feasibility >2 weeks**: Diminishing returns on analysis, decide
- **Stuck in Decision Pending >1 week**: Analysis paralysis, force a decision

### Forcing Decisions
If an idea lingers too long:
1. Set hard deadline (this Friday)
2. List what you know vs. what you don't
3. Decide with available data
4. Document assumptions and uncertainties
5. Move to go/no-go/on-hold

"Perfect information doesn't exist. Decide with what you have."

## Tips for Portfolio Success

1. **Capture freely, research selectively**: Write down every idea, but only research the promising ones
2. **Default to no-go**: Protect your time. Only pursue high-conviction opportunities.
3. **Extract lessons always**: Every decision is data. Build pattern recognition.
4. **Move through stages**: Don't linger. Research → Decide → Next.
5. **Revisit periodically**: On-hold ideas may become viable. Rejected ideas may have pivots.
6. **Track honestly**: Don't fudge the data. Honest tracking improves future decisions.
7. **Celebrate both go and no-go**: A good no-go decision is as valuable as a go. It saves time and resources.
