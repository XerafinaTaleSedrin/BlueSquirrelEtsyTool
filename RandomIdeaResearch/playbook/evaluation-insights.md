## Research Methodology Insights

### What Works in Market Research

*How to research markets more effectively*

**Example insights** (add your own as you learn):
- Start with broad Google search, then narrow to specific competitors
- Reddit discussions more valuable than formal market reports for validation
- Product review sites (G2, Capterra) reveal real pain points vs. marketing claims
- Google Trends shows direction, not just magnitude
- Talking to one potential customer > reading ten blog posts

---

### What Works in Feasibility Analysis

*How to assess viability more accurately*

**Example insights**:
- Always add 50-100% buffer to time estimates
- Get actual quotes from services (hosting, APIs) vs. guessing costs
- Look at comparable projects for realistic complexity assessment
- Revenue estimates should use pessimistic conversion rates (1-2%, not 5-10%)
- Personal fit score often most predictive of actual pursuit

## Decision-Making Insights

### What Makes Decisions Easier

*How to decide with confidence*

**Example insights**:
- Clear red flags make no-go obvious (don't need perfect data)
- When excited after research (not before), strong go signal
- Scoring forces honest assessment vs. vague "seems good"
- Writing decision reasoning clarifies thinking
- Low confidence + go = start with small validation experiments

---

### What Makes Decisions Harder

*When to recognize analysis paralysis*

**Example insights**:
- Researching for >2 weeks often means not compelling
- Constantly changing idea to fix problems = original idea wasn't viable
- Waiting for "perfect" information prevents decision
- If can't answer "why now?" clearly, timing probably wrong
- Oscillating between go/no-go = mediocre idea, default to no

---

## Time Efficiency Insights

### How to Research Faster Without Sacrificing Quality

*Optimize time investment*

**Example insights**:
- Set research time-boxes (4 hours market, 2 hours feasibility)
- Use specialized agents for heavy lifting
- Don't research until ready to potentially act on it
- Template structure speeds up documentation
- Parallel research (market + feasibility) if skilled at both

---

### When to Stop Research

*Knowing when you have enough information*

**Example insights**:
- If 5+ competitors found and all similar, market is saturated (stop)
- If can't find evidence problem exists after 1 hour searching, likely doesn't
- When all research confirms initial suspicion, don't need more data
- Clear deal-breaker = stop immediately, don't finish rest
- When pattern matches playbook red flags strongly, stop

## Assumption Validation Insights

### What Assumptions to Test First

*Prioritize validation*

**Example insights**:
- Willingness to pay > market size (revenue matters more than TAM)
- Customer accessibility > customer count (can you reach them?)
- Technical feasibility > technical elegance (does it work > is it perfect)
- "Why hasn't this been done?" > all other questions
- Personal fit > market opportunity (better to pursue smaller opportunity you fit)

---

### How to Validate Assumptions Cheaply

*Test before building*

**Example insights**:
- Landing page with pricing > survey about willingness to pay
- 5 customer conversations > 500 responses to survey
- Working prototype > detailed spec
- Try to buy competitors' product > read about it
- Attempt to pre-sell > build and hope

## Scoring Calibration Insights

### How Scores Correlate to Outcomes

*What scores predict what outcomes*

*This section grows as you pursue ideas and see results*

**Example insights you might discover**:
- Ideas scoring <3.0 overall never pursued (threshold works)
- Personal Fit <3 leads to abandonment even if other scores high
- Any 1/5 dimension has always been fatal flaw
- Two dimensions at 5/5 stronger signal than five at 3/5
- When gut says no but scores say yes, gut usually right (update scoring)

## Information Source Insights

### Most Valuable Research Sources

*Which sources provide best signal*

**Example insights**:
- Reddit > formal market reports (honest pain points)
- Product reviews > marketing websites (real experience)
- Failed startup post-mortems > success stories (learn what doesn't work)
- Competitor pricing pages > all other financial data
- Google Trends > one-time market size estimates (direction matters)

---

### Least Valuable Research Sources

*What to skip or minimize*

**Example insights**:
- Generic startup advice blogs (not specific enough)
- Overly optimistic market size projections
- Old industry reports (>2 years old)
- Founder interviews (selection bias toward successes)
- Generic "how to start a business" content

---

## Mistake Patterns

### Common Evaluation Mistakes

*What errors to avoid*

**Example insights**:
- Confirmation bias: Only researching positive signals
- Sunk cost: Continuing because spent time researching
- Optimism bias: Using best-case estimates
- Comparison to outliers: "But Airbnb/Uber started small..."
- Feature creep: Adding features to fix problems with core idea
- Analysis paralysis: Researching instead of testing
- Overweighting single data point: One customer interview ≠ market validation

*Best practices for delegating to agents*

**Example insights**:

- Feasibility-analyzer needs market research completed first
- Decision-framework-builder should synthesize, not generate new research

## Decision Confidence Insights

### What Confidence Levels Mean

*Interpreting and acting on confidence*

**Example insights you might discover**:
- High confidence + go = pursue immediately
- Medium confidence + go = start with validation experiments
- Low confidence + go = red flag (probably shouldn't go)
- High confidence + no-go = clear decision, move on quickly
- Low confidence + no-go = still right decision (when in doubt, say no)
